# America's Next Top Model

LLMs are an interesting technology. I'm a self-taught programmer, something that always gave me imposter syndrome. Maybe learning it systematically at school really does have big advantages over tinkered self-learning. But if the script were flipped, the fact that I went to school for supply chain never has me thinking someone who's tinkered repeatedly knows less than I do; and it's quite possible that it's the other way around. If someone *does* something supply chain related, especially repeatedly, they have no other choice but to learn its intricacies; possibly better than someone who only half-paid attention in school. So the *learning is in the doing*. With code, LLMs completely change what it means *to do*.

Sure to improve in a variet of ways over the years, LLMs have already in their current state made the barrier to entry miniscule. So what does this do to programming? While talking to a friend, I found myself drawing the analogy to music production. As a teenager in the 2000s, I was in awe of music production software. I couldn't play any instruments for production-grade music, though I took sitar and piano classes as a kid. But I could click about on a computer and produce fun-sounding music. Previously a niche category, electronic music exploded and became a category of its own. Decades later, I argue that most music nowadays is produced and recorded electronically even if it *sounds* analog.

What I noticed as I tinkered my way into a few mildly popular songs and remixes, was that there was a section of the audience who simply didn't care about the details I am talking about. To the person on the street, if it sounded good, it was good music. But as a few of my experiments gained popularity, I noticed the *experts* sometimes claim what I and others like me were doing wasn't real music. Traditional DJs would say modern DJs are not *actually, truly* mixing music. Classical musicians and people who played instruments would hint at my inability to actually play any instruments. "DJs nowadays just press play and pause" was a big meme, maybe it still is. So there was a similar discussion as LLMs on what it means to be a real, actual musician; versus live-action-role-playing with a computer program. I didn't have too much of an opinion, I never considered myself a serious musician. I was in it for the fun.

Fun along the way is important. Very important.

My teenage experience taught me something useful: that criticisms were simply future problems waiting to be solved. The fact that CDs and MP3s were lower quality than vinyls, that a computer program could never be as good as human taste or aesthetic choices, that this new technology was responsible for the lack of appreciation of the artisans of the craft, are all parallel comments to what I hear about LLMs nowadays. Eventually, music could be streamed losslessly, the music mixing and production software got ridiculously better, and at some point in time, the newschoolers became the old school.

> Criticisms aren't a reason to stop tinkering. They're pointers aimed at problems to solve.

Fast forward, I tinkered about this morning and added an audio/video upload feature to this blog. It's very cool to me that I can type features into existence. It's something I could've only dreamed of doing without computer programs. I'm uploading my first ever audio note, let's see if it works. Play/pause all over again?
