# America's Next Top Model

LLMs are an interesting technology. I am a self-taught programmer, something that always gave me imposter syndrome. Maybe learning it systematically really does have big advantages over trial-and-error-based learning. But if the script is reversed, the fact that *I* went to school for supply chain never has me thinking someone who simply watched YouTube videos knows less than I do. If someone does something supply chain related, they have no other choice but to learn about it. So the learning is in doing. With code, LLMs completely change what it means to do. They're new tech still, and sure to improve over the next years, but they already make the barrier to entry miniscule.

So what does this do to programming? While talking to a friend, I found myself drawing the analogy of music production. In the 2000s as a teenager, I was in awe of music production software. I couldn't play any instruments for production-grade music, though I took sitar and piano classes as a kid. But I could click about on a computer and produce music. Electronic music became a category of its own, though I would argue that most music nowadays is produced electronically even if it sounds analog.

What I noticed as I trial'd and error'd my way into a few mildly popular songs and remixes, was that there was a section of the audience who simply didn't care about the details. If it sounded good, it was good music. Those were the end-users and customers of the product. But as a few of my experiments gained popularity, I noticed the "experts" sometimes claim what I was doing wasn't real music. Traditional DJs would say I am not *actually, truly* mixing music. The instrument players or classical musicians would hint at my inability to actually play music. "DJs nowadays just press play and pause" was a big meme, maybe still is. So there was a similar discussion on what it means to be a real, actual musician, versus live-action-role-playing with a computer program. I didn't have too much of an opinion, I never considered myself a serious musician, and was only in it for the fun.

But that experience taught me something useful. That the criticisms are simply problems to be solved, and they will be in time. That CDs and mp3s were lower quality than vinyls, that a computer program could never be as good as human taste, that this new technology was responsible for the lack of appreciation of the artisans of the craft, are all parallel comments to what I hear about LLMs nowadays. Eventually, music could be streamed losslessly, the music mixing and production got ridiculously better, and at some point in time, the newschoolers became the old school. The loop repeats.

> I don't think criticisms are a reason to stop tinkering. On the contrary, they are pointers aimed at the next problems to solve.
